## OVERVIEW: WHY A TV SHOW

Before diving into formats, budgets, and episode breakdowns, it is worth stating plainly why a television property is not a marketing expense for DealStage — it is a core product line.

The creator economy is a $250 billion market growing at 30% annually. It has no cultural home. Shark Tank owns the startup investment narrative. The Voice owns the talent discovery narrative. Love Is Blind owns the relationship stakes narrative. Nobody owns the creator-brand partnership narrative. Not yet.

DealStage is the first company positioned to own that cultural moment — and television is the mechanism that transforms a software platform into a cultural institution. Every episode of a DealStage show does five things simultaneously: it entertains, it educates, it demonstrates the platform's capabilities in real time, it recruits new users, and it builds the brand equity that no paid acquisition budget can manufacture.

Two formats are presented here. They are not competing concepts — they are a sequenced strategy. Version B (the network game show) can launch first as a lower-budget, broader-audience proof of concept. Version A (the premium streaming series) is the flagship that follows when the concept has demonstrated mainstream appeal. Both can run simultaneously on different platforms, targeting different audience segments, with the same underlying brand identity.

**Production Model:** High-production-value reality/competition hybrid
**Comparable Shows:** Shark Tank, The Voice, The Apprentice, Making the Band

## THE CORE CONCEPT

Shark Tank made deals entertaining by making the investment moment dramatic. The Voice made competition emotionally resonant by centering it on genuine human talent. The Deal Stage does both — and adds a third dimension that neither of those shows has ever had: data transparency.

Twelve rising creators — each with between 50,000 and 500,000 followers across their primary platform — arrive at The Deal Stage with one goal: land the biggest brand deal of their career. Over ten episodes, they face real brand challenges issued by Fortune 500 companies and fast-growing challenger brands. They must research, strategize, pitch, negotiate, and close. They are helped — and sometimes humbled — by DealStage AI, whose match scores, audience intelligence, and deal recommendations are revealed publicly, creating a layer of drama and strategy that no reality competition has ever employed before.

The contestant who closes the $1 million partnership deal at the end of Episode 10 does not just win money. They walk away with a deal that is real, binding, and career-defining. The show is not a simulation. The stakes are not theatrical. The money is actual.

This is the difference that makes The Deal Stage unprecedented: it is a competition show where the prize is not given by judges. It is earned through performance that a brand — a real company with a real budget — has decided is worth paying for.

## FULL DEMOGRAPHICS BREAKDOWN — VERSION A

### Quantitative Profile

- **Primary Age Range:** 22-38
- **Secondary Age Range:** 39-54 (business-interested viewers, parents of creators)
- **Gender Split:** 52% female, 48% male
- **Household Income:** $60,000-$175,000
- **Education:** 65% college-educated or higher
- **Geography:** Top markets are New York, Los Angeles, Chicago, Atlanta, Austin, Miami, Seattle, and Nashville — the same cities where creator economy activity is highest
- **Platform Behavior:** 78% have an active Instagram or TikTok account; 61% follow at least one creator they consider a trusted voice in a niche; 44% have purchased a product because a creator recommended it in the last 30 days

### Psychographic Profile

This audience does not watch television passively. They watch with a second screen in their hand, and that second screen is actively engaged. They are aspirational — many of them either are creators themselves, have considered becoming creators, work in marketing or adjacent fields, or are deeply embedded in the business of the creator economy as professionals.

They are drawn to shows where the competition has real-world stakes and the contestants have genuine expertise that can be evaluated. They respected the expertise demonstrated on Shark Tank and Queer Eye. They hated when competition shows felt rigged or outcome-predetermined. They will engage with The Deal Stage because the AI scoring system provides an objective layer of accountability — when the brand makes a decision, viewers can compare it against the data and have an opinion.

This audience also has a high clip-sharing behavior. The moments that perform on social media are not emotional reunion scenes — they are the moments of revealed information: the score going up, the counter-offer being accepted, the brand switching its preference unexpectedly. This audience creates the viral engine that sustains the show between episodes.

### Secondary Audience: The Creator Community Itself

There are approximately 50 million people worldwide who self-identify as content creators. Of those, roughly 2 million in the United States have at least 10,000 followers on a primary platform. Every single one of those people has a professional interest in what The Deal Stage is teaching and showing. They watch not just for entertainment but for business intelligence: what brands are paying, what pitches are working, what DealStage AI is seeing in the data that they cannot see themselves.

This secondary audience is the most powerful distribution mechanism the show has. When a contestant shares a clip of their AI match score being revealed, their audience — which is already primed to care about that creator's career — watches. When a contestant loses and talks about what the data showed about why, their audience learns something and shares it. The show has a built-in network of 12 content creators each promoting it to their combined audiences throughout the season.

## EPISODE 1: FULL SCRIPT OUTLINE

### "THE ARRIVAL" — Pilot Episode

**Cold Open: 0:00 — 3:00**

No host introduction. No title card. The show opens on twelve individual screens — twelve smartphones, twelve creator profiles, twelve social stats flashing on the screen like vital signs on a hospital monitor. We see follower counts, engagement rates, best-performing content clips. A voiceover — low, measured, authoritative — speaks:

_"In 2026, the creator economy is worth $250 billion. Brands spend more on creator partnerships than they spend on traditional television advertising. And yet most creators — the ones building real audiences, real trust, real communities — have no idea what they're worth."_

Cut to rapid-fire interview clips: twelve contestants, shot in handheld close-up, each answering the same question in two seconds: "What's the biggest deal you've ever closed?"

The answers range from $500 to $85,000. One says she's never closed a deal at all.

_"For the next ten weeks, twelve creators will compete for the largest brand partnership deal in the history of this show. One million dollars. One deal. Zero simulations. Everything you are about to see is real."_

The Deal Stage title sequence fires: bold typography, AI data visualizations morphing into deal contracts morphing into creator content clips, scored by a driving original composition that signals intelligence and ambition.

**Contestant Introductions: 3:00 — 8:00**

The twelve contestants arrive at The Deal Stage compound — a converted industrial creative space in downtown Los Angeles designed to look like the intersection of a startup workspace and a broadcast studio. The architecture matters: the space communicates that this is where business gets done, not where people sit in ornate chairs waiting to be judged.

Contestants are introduced in groups of three, with fast cuts between arrival moments and individual confessional interviews:

**Group One: The Established Rising Stars**

- **Marcus, 29, Atlanta** — 485,000 YouTube subscribers, personal finance for Black professionals. Has done one paid deal. Makes less per video than he makes at his day job.
- **Priya, 31, New York** — 320,000 Instagram followers, sustainable fashion. Has multiple deals but none above $8,000. Knows her audience is valuable; has never been paid like it is.
- **Tyler, 26, Austin** — 290,000 TikTok, comedy/lifestyle. Viral clips regularly but never converted into consistent brand income. Pitch inexperienced.

**Group Two: The Specialists**

- **Simone, 34, Chicago** — 180,000 across platforms, wellness and mental health for working mothers. Deeply loyal audience, unmonetized.
- **Carlos, 28, Miami** — 220,000 Instagram, sneaker culture and streetwear. Already doing deals but they are underpaying him and he knows it.
- **Anya, 33, Seattle** — 155,000 YouTube, B2B SaaS content and tech product reviews. Niche, high-income audience. Brands are not finding her.

**Group Three: The Underdogs**

- **Devon, 24, Houston** — 62,000 TikTok, gaming and esports. Youngest contestant. The audience wonders if he belongs here.
- **Naomi, 30, Los Angeles** — 88,000 Instagram, travel and hospitality. Has the lifestyle but not yet the scale brands typically require.
- **Jordan, 27, Nashville** — 110,000 YouTube, independent music production. Operates in a niche that brands rarely target but his audience spending power is exceptionally high.

**Group Four: The Veterans**

- **Keisha, 36, Washington D.C.** — 420,000 across LinkedIn and Instagram, corporate career development. Has rejected three deals this year because they felt wrong. Has never been offered the right one.
- **Sam, 31, Portland** — 275,000 podcast listeners plus YouTube, environmental entrepreneurship. The most articulate pitcher in the group. Has never pitched a brand in a competitive context.
- **Leila, 29, Phoenix** — 195,000 TikTok and YouTube, Latina beauty and skincare. Has the most brand deal experience of the group, none above $15,000.

Each introduction runs approximately 25 seconds — enough to establish the person, their platform, their stakes, and their personality. Viewers should be able to predict in this segment who they will love and who they will root against, while knowing that the data will eventually complicate all of those judgments.

**The DealStage Reveal: 8:00 — 12:00**

The host — positioned to be a name personality from the business/media world; targets include Sarah Cooper, Gary Vaynerchuk (cameo-style), Issa Rae, or a casting-developed original host — walks the contestants into The Data Room for the first time.

The Data Room is the architectural centerpiece of The Deal Stage set: a circular space with floor-to-ceiling screens displaying live DealStage AI visualizations. Each contestant's profile is shown simultaneously — their match score, audience data, content performance index, and Deal Score — all visible to everyone in the room.

This is the first moment of real vulnerability. The scores are not hidden. The data is not private. Every contestant sees every other contestant's numbers in real time.

The host explains the rules: over ten episodes, contestants will face real brand challenges. They will use DealStage to research, build their pitches, and present to real brand decision-makers. AI match scores will be revealed publicly. At the end of each episode, the brand selects who advances, who is at risk, and who goes home.

The host pauses. "Before we bring in your first brand partner, I want you to look at that screen."

All twelve match scores are displayed. The room is silent.

**Brand Partner Reveal: Spotify**

A senior Spotify executive — the VP of Creator Partnerships — walks into The Data Room. The contestants react to the brand reveal with genuine emotion: some with recognition and excitement, some with nerves about whether they are right for it.

The Spotify brief is explained on screen and in person:

- Spotify is expanding its creator partnership program and is looking for a face for their new "Independent Voices" campaign.
- The campaign requires a creator with an authentic relationship with music or audio culture — but the definition is deliberately broad.
- The deal available in this episode is a $75,000 partnership for a three-month activation across the creator's platforms.
- Spotify is not looking for the biggest following. They are looking for the deepest resonance.

The executive pauses before leaving the room: "We'll be watching."

**Research Phase — DealStage in Action: 12:00 — 20:00**

The contestants are given 60 minutes with full DealStage platform access to research Spotify, build their pitch strategy, and prepare a three-minute presentation. The clock runs on screen. The Data Room screens show real-time activity — who is searching what, which data points are being pulled, how each contestant's DealStage Match Score with Spotify is shifting as the platform processes more information about their fit.

This segment is constructed through fast-cut editing between four concurrent storylines:

**Marcus** pulls his audience demographic data and realizes something the Spotify executive might not know yet: 71% of his viewers watch his finance videos with music playing in the background. His content and Spotify's core product have a natural passive consumption alignment. He builds his pitch around "the soundtrack to financial freedom."

**Devon** — the youngest, with the smallest following — discovers through DealStage that his gaming audience has a 340% above-average index for Spotify's podcast consumption. His audience does not just listen to music on Spotify. They listen to hours of audio content weekly. His DealStage Match Score with Spotify climbs from 58 to 74 during the research phase, the highest single-session improvement of any contestant.

**Leila** has a natural case — she creates beauty content with heavy music integration. But DealStage reveals that three other creators with her audience profile have already done Spotify campaigns, and their performance metrics were mediocre. Her pitch needs a differentiation angle. She finds it: her audience skews heavily toward first-generation Latina listeners who are underrepresented in Spotify's creator partnerships. Authenticity of representation becomes her angle.

**Priya** discovers her match score with Spotify is only 51 — below average. DealStage shows why: her content is primarily visual and text-based; her audience consumes very little audio. Rather than pretend this does not exist, she decides to address it directly in her pitch. "I know my match score says 51. Here is why that number is wrong."

**Pitch Presentations: 20:00 — 30:00**

The first four contestants to pitch are selected by the producers for maximum contrast: Marcus, Devon, Leila, and Priya. The Spotify executive is joined by two panelists — a talent manager from a major agency and the host — who observe but do not interrupt.

Each contestant has three minutes at the presentation podium. Their DealStage profile is visible on the screen behind them the entire time. As they speak, new data points can be requested to appear — a contestant can say "show my engagement rate by content type" and the screen updates in real time. The data is a tool and a witness simultaneously.

**Marcus** opens with his discovery about background listening. He shows Spotify's own data — pulled via DealStage's integration with publicly available platform research — on the correlation between passive audio consumption and subscription conversion. His pitch is confident, data-backed, and positions him not just as a creator but as a channel strategy insight. He ends with a proposed campaign structure: a 12-week series called "The Soundtrack to Financial Freedom," with one Spotify-integrated video per week and a custom playlist he would curate for his audience. He quotes a rate 20% above what Spotify offered. Time: 2 minutes 47 seconds.

**Devon** walks up nervous. The Spotify executive glances at his follower count — 62,000 — and shifts almost imperceptibly in her chair. Devon sees it. He opens with it: "I know what you're thinking. Sixty-two thousand. Why is he up here?" Then he puts his DealStage match score on the screen: 74. The highest in the room. He explains what it means — his audience listens to more Spotify content per week than any other contestant's audience by a significant margin. He is not selling reach. He is selling depth of relationship with the exact user Spotify needs to retain. His pitch is rough around the edges but the data is overwhelming. He ends with one line: "My audience doesn't just use Spotify. They live in it." Time: 2 minutes 33 seconds.

**Leila** leads with her differentiation angle — the underrepresentation insight. She acknowledges her match score is above average but not the highest in the room, and she pivots to something DealStage cannot quantify: the cultural significance of authentic Latina representation in audio content. She shows her audience breakdown — 68% Latina, 55% bilingual, 40% of whom consume content in both English and Spanish. She pitches a bilingual activation. Time: 3 minutes exactly.

**Priya** is last and her opening is the moment the episode has been building toward. She calls her own match score of 51 onto the screen herself. "Let's talk about this number." She walks through exactly why DealStage's AI flagged her as a below-average fit — her content is visual-first, her audience is not audio-heavy. Then she makes her counter-argument: her audience trusts her product recommendations more than any other creator in the room, based on a third-party engagement quality metric she found buried in her DealStage dashboard. She pitches Spotify not as a music integration but as a platform recommendation from a trusted voice. "I am not your average Spotify partner. That is exactly why you should choose me." Time: 2 minutes 58 seconds.

**AI Match Scores Revealed — The Data Drama: 30:00 — 38:00**

The host steps forward. "Before the Spotify team deliberates, DealStage is going to do something that has never happened on a television show before. We are going to show you — all of you, and everyone watching at home — exactly what the AI thinks about each pitch."

The screens update. DealStage's post-pitch analysis compares each contestant's pre-pitch score against their post-pitch performance rating — a measure of how effectively they used the platform's data, how their pitch aligned with the brand brief, and how accurately they represented their audience data.

The results are surprising:

- Devon's score climbs to 79 — the highest post-pitch score, despite the smallest following
- Marcus holds steady at 73 — a strong showing, the AI confirms his data usage was sophisticated
- Leila drops slightly to 68 — her cultural angle was compelling but her proposed activation has structural issues the AI flagged
- Priya's post-pitch score is 64 — higher than her match score, showing the AI credited her for addressing her own weakness directly

The Spotify executive has been watching the scores update in real time. Her expression throughout this segment is the most closely watched footage of the episode. She did not expect the youngest contestant with the smallest following to produce the highest data-supported score.

**Judge Deliberation: 38:00 — 45:00**

The three judges — the Spotify VP, the talent manager, and the host — deliberate in a semi-private space with microphones and cameras present. The conversation is genuine and unscripted. The Spotify VP is visibly torn between Devon's data and Leila's cultural pitch. The talent manager argues for Priya's self-awareness as a professional quality that outlasts any single campaign. The host notes that Marcus's pitch was the most immediately executable.

The deliberation surfaces a tension that will run through the entire season: does the brand choose what the data recommends, or does human judgment override the AI? There is no predetermined answer. That tension is the philosophical engine of the show.

**Results: 45:00 — 52:00**

The four pitching contestants stand in the Data Room. The remaining eight watch from the observation tier.

The Spotify VP delivers the verdict:

- **Advancing to the next round of consideration:** Devon and Marcus
- **At risk, must improve:** Leila — her cultural angle is compelling but her activation plan needs to be significantly strengthened before the brand would commit
- **Going home:** Priya — not because her pitch was wrong, but because the brand needs an audio-native creator for this campaign, and her data cannot be overcome by self-awareness alone

Priya's elimination is handled with genuine respect. The Spotify VP tells her: "Your ability to use data against yourself is one of the smartest things I've seen in a pitch room. You have the wrong product for my brand right now. That does not mean you have the wrong skills for this industry. Come back to me with a visual-first brand and we'll talk."

**Eliminated Contestant Farewell: 52:00 — 58:00**

Priya's exit interview is one of the episode's emotional centerpieces. She is not devastated — she is reflective. She talks about what the DealStage platform showed her about herself that she did not know before walking into the building. She commits on camera to applying what she learned. The show's thesis — that data intelligence makes better creators, not just better pitched deals — is embodied in her departure.

The other contestants react. Devon is visibly moved. Leila is recalculating.

**Preview of Episode 2: 58:00 — 60:00**

The screens in the Data Room activate. A new brand logo fades in. The contestants lean forward. The Episode 1 brand has revealed a second tier of the challenge. A voice announces: "Next week, your brand isn't a platform. It's a product. And this one has opinions about who represents it."

## FULL SEASON ARC — ALL 10 EPISODES

### Episode 1: "The Arrival"

Brand Partner: Spotify. Individual challenges. Establish all twelve contestants, reveal the DealStage Data Room, first AI score reveal. One contestant eliminated.

### Episode 2: "The Product Test"

Brand Partner: Nike. Contestants must not just pitch but demonstrate the product in content they create during the episode. Introduces the content creation element — contestants now have to be both strategic and performative. Two contestants at risk. One eliminated.

### Episode 3: "The Niche"

Brand Partner: Notion (the productivity platform). This episode deliberately favors creators with smaller, more specialized audiences. Devon and Anya — the two lowest-follower contestants — are front-runners for the first time. The episode establishes that follower count is not the determinant of value. One eliminated. After three eliminations, nine contestants remain.

### Episode 4: "The Bundle"

First team challenge. Contestants are paired by DealStage AI into teams based on complementary audience profiles. Brand Partner: American Express. The brief requires a co-created campaign from two creators whose combined audiences deliver something neither can deliver alone. Teams must negotiate internally before pitching externally. One team eliminated (both members out).

### Episode 5: "The Pitch War"

Brand Partner: Samsung. Teams continue but this time the teams are self-selected — contestants can choose their partner. The DealStage AI publicly shows who has the best potential synergy with whom. Some contestants ignore the data. Those who ignore it pay a price. One team eliminated.

### Episode 6: "The Comeback"

The Wildcard episode. Between Episodes 5 and 6, eliminated contestants have had the opportunity to go close a real brand deal in the outside world — any brand, any deal — and bring documentation of the closed contract back. Any eliminated contestant who successfully closed a real deal can apply to re-enter. Historically, two eliminated contestants return each season (this is established in season one as the intended format rule). The re-entry is voted on by the remaining contestants using DealStage data — they must argue for or against a comeback based on the quality of the deal the eliminated contestant closed, not personal preference.

### Episode 7: "The Big Room"

Brand Partner: A major automotive brand (Chevrolet or Ford as target). The budget for this deal is $250,000 — three times the previous highest. The stakes shift visibly. The pitches are more formal, longer, and more heavily data-dependent. Contestants who have been coasting on charm begin to crack. One eliminated.

### Episode 8: "The Negotiation"

Brand Partner: A major beauty or wellness brand (Sephora, Glossier, or Fenty Beauty as targets). This episode introduces the negotiation element in its most complex form: the brand comes in with an opening offer, and contestants must counter, justify the counter with DealStage data, and negotiate in real time. The contestant who accepts the first offer without countering is eliminated — not for losing, but for failing to advocate for themselves. This rule is announced at the start of the episode. One eliminated.

### Episode 9: "The Semi-Final"

Final four contestants. Brand Partner: A tech company with significant brand cachet (Apple, Google, or Microsoft as targets). The deal prize at stake is $500,000. One contestant eliminated. Final three advance to the finale.

### Episode 10: "The Deal"

The Finale. Three contestants. Brand Partner revealed at the top of the episode — kept secret until broadcast. The deal prize is $1,000,000. Live audience of 500 in a flagship venue — either the Hollywood Palladium, Radio City Music Hall, or an equivalent landmark. The finale incorporates a live audience vote component: viewers watching at home can vote via the DealStage app for which contestant's pitch they found most compelling. This vote does not override the brand's decision — but it is shared with the brand before they make their final choice, and it is publicly announced. The gap between audience preference and brand choice — when it exists — is one of the most discussed moments of the season.

## TWISTS AND FORMAT INNOVATIONS — DETAILED

### 1. The Data Room

The Data Room is not a segment — it is the show's central architectural element. Every episode begins and ends there. When contestants are in The Data Room, their complete DealStage profiles are visible on the screens. There is nowhere to hide. A contestant cannot claim their engagement rate is higher than it is, cannot exaggerate their audience demographics, cannot pitch a fake audience persona. The data is the floor of every conversation and the ceiling of every bluff.

For viewers, The Data Room creates a new kind of competition dynamic: they have the same information the brand has. They can form their own opinions before the decision is announced. This transforms passive watching into active prediction, which is the engine of second-screen engagement.

### 2. Match Score Transparency

In every other competition format, judging criteria are partially subjective and partially opaque. The AI Match Score is neither. When DealStage displays a score, it displays the component factors simultaneously: audience alignment percentage, content category fit, engagement quality index, previous brand performance history, audience spending power index. Viewers can see not just the score but why the score is what it is.

This creates strategy and drama simultaneously. A contestant with a low match score for a given brand has two choices: accept the disadvantage or attack one of the component factors in their pitch. The contestants who learn to attack the AI's assumptions — and succeed — become the season's heroes. The ones who ignore the data and lose to someone who used it are the cautionary tales.

### 3. The Clock

When contestants enter their research phase, the clock is on screen. Sixty minutes. No extensions. When the clock reaches zero, DealStage locks and the pitch preparation is over. The final ten minutes of every research phase are conducted under visible time pressure, and the editing of those final minutes is designed to extract maximum tension from the combination of data analysis and imminent performance.

### 4. The Brand Switch

In one episode (target: Episode 7 or 8), the brand changes the brief mid-challenge. The original brief is issued, contestants research and prepare, and then — thirty minutes into their sixty-minute preparation window — the brand representative returns to announce a significant change: different target audience, different campaign objective, different budget. Contestants must adapt in real time. DealStage updates. Scores shift. Strategies built in the first thirty minutes may become liabilities. The episode demonstrates what real brand partnership relationships actually require: flexibility, composure, and the ability to use data quickly under pressure.

### 5. The Comeback

The Comeback rule is not just a dramatic device — it is the show's statement about how the creator economy actually works. Success is not determined by a single pitch in a competition setting. It is determined by the ability to close deals in the real world. An eliminated contestant who can walk back in with a signed contract — any contract — has demonstrated exactly the skill the show is trying to identify. The Comeback episode is structurally positioned to be one of the most emotionally complex of the season because it forces the remaining contestants to evaluate someone they eliminated through an objective data lens.

## 10 SPECIFIC BRAND PARTNERS — VERSION A

### 1. Spotify

**Why they say yes:** Spotify has invested heavily in its creator economy positioning (podcasts, audiobooks, creator tools). The show provides six to ten episodes of brand integration at a cost that is competitive with a single major campaign buy. More importantly, the show allows Spotify to demonstrate — in front of a business-literate audience — that it understands creators and values data-informed partnerships.

**Integration level:** Title sponsor of the Research Phase segment ("The Spotify Research Window"). Episode 1 featured brand.

**Estimated fee:** $3M for season-wide integration plus the Episode 1 deal prize ($75,000).

### 2. Nike

**Why they say yes:** Nike has been recalibrating its creator strategy since pulling back from traditional advertising. The brand has publicly stated its interest in authentic creator relationships over celebrity endorsements. The Deal Stage provides a platform where Nike can be positioned as a discerning brand that knows what it is looking for — aligning with their "we don't sponsor everyone" identity.

**Integration level:** Episode 2 featured brand. Product challenge gives Nike organic product exposure.

**Estimated fee:** $2.5M for episode integration plus deal prize.

### 3. American Express

**Why they say yes:** AmEx has always marketed itself as a brand for people who are serious about their money and their ambitions. Their Amex Offers program is already creator-adjacent. A team challenge episode where creators must bundle their value propositions mirrors exactly what AmEx values: sophisticated partnership thinking, not transactional promotion.

**Integration level:** Episode 4 team challenge featured brand.

**Estimated fee:** $2M for episode integration plus deal prize.

### 4. Sephora

**Why they say yes:** Beauty is the largest category in creator-brand partnerships. Sephora has an existing influencer program but has publicly struggled with the authenticity question — too many of their creators feel interchangeable. The Deal Stage provides Sephora with a televised, data-validated process for identifying genuinely high-value creator partnerships that they can then point to as evidence of rigor.

**Integration level:** Episode 8 featured brand.

**Estimated fee:** $2.5M for episode integration plus deal prize.

### 5. Notion

**Why they say yes:** Notion has built its brand on creator and community ambassador marketing. Their entire growth story is creator-first. A competition show that rewards intelligent use of tools and data is a brand alignment that Notion's marketing team will immediately recognize. The episode that features a small-follower specialist winning because of data sophistication — rather than scale — is exactly the story Notion wants to tell about its own user base.

**Integration level:** Episode 3 featured brand. The research phase in this episode features a Notion integration within DealStage's workspace tools.

**Estimated fee:** $1.5M for episode integration plus deal prize ($50,000, appropriate for the niche-audience episode).

### 6. Samsung

**Why they say yes:** Samsung has one of the most aggressive creator marketing budgets in consumer electronics. They are a natural TV show sponsor. More specifically, Samsung's Galaxy creator program is directly competitive with Apple's similar initiative, and a prime-time competition series that shows Samsung as the technology enabling creators to succeed positions them favorably in that rivalry.

**Integration level:** Episode 5 featured brand. Devices used throughout the show are Samsung-branded.

**Estimated fee:** $3.5M for season-wide device integration plus Episode 5 deal prize.

### 7. Cash App / Block

**Why they say yes:** Cash App's entire brand identity is built around the creator and independent earner. Their sponsorship of musicians, athletes, and creators is a defining feature of their marketing strategy. The Deal Stage is the most natural brand alignment in their portfolio — a show about creators earning money that is fundamentally about financial empowerment.

**Integration level:** Segment sponsor of the "Deal Closing" sequence in every episode. When a deal is accepted, the transaction animation is Cash App-branded.

**Estimated fee:** $2M for season-wide segment sponsorship.

### 8. HubSpot

**Why they say yes:** HubSpot has built the most effective content marketing operation in B2B SaaS. They have a massive media business (YouTube channel, podcast, newsletter) and they understand the value of entertainment as a marketing channel better than almost any other B2B brand. Sponsoring The Deal Stage gives HubSpot a B2C-adjacent reach that their platform cannot generate organically, while the show's data-and-intelligence theme is a direct brand alignment.

**Integration level:** Presenting sponsor of "The Data Room." Data visualizations carry subtle HubSpot co-branding.

**Estimated fee:** $1.5M for Data Room sponsorship.

**Format:** 20-episode per season weekly game show

## THE CORE CONCEPT

Where Version A is a season-long character study of twelve people navigating the complexity of professional creator partnerships, Version B is a different animal entirely. It is fast. It is accessible. It is designed to work for a viewer who has never heard of the creator economy and does not care about DealStage specifically — but who loves the moment when someone has to decide whether to accept a deal or push for more.

The game show format has one irreplaceable asset: the decision moment. The $1,000,000 briefcase opening. The contestant deciding whether to take the bank's offer. These are among the most-watched moments in television history because they are psychologically universal — everyone understands the fear and excitement of a high-stakes choice. Version B is built entirely around engineering that moment within the context of a creator brand deal.

The twist — the element that makes this different from any game show that has existed before — is that the AI tells you what to do. DealStage's algorithm analyzes the brand brief, the contestant's audience data, and market comparable rates, and it gives a specific recommendation: accept, counter at this specific amount, or walk away. The contestant can follow the AI. The contestant can ignore it. Both choices have consequences. The audience watches to see which choice they make and whether the AI was right.

## EPISODE FORMAT — DETAILED

**Total Runtime:** 30 minutes
**Commercial Breaks:** 3 breaks, standard broadcast format
**Studio:** 300-person live studio audience
**Set:** "The Deal Stage" — a physical podium on a raised platform, surrounded by large screens displaying DealStage AI data. The AI is visualized as an entity with a distinct aesthetic — not a robot, not a human, but a data environment that feels alive.

---

### COLD OPEN (0:00 — 1:30)

The host enters to the studio audience. Brief recap of the show premise — delivered by the host in 30 seconds, designed to orient first-time viewers. The three contestants for today's episode are introduced in rapid sequence: name, platform, follower count, shown on the screens behind them. The studio audience applauds. The brand partner for today's episode is revealed: a logo, a product, a brief description of what they are looking for.

This cold open is identical in structure every episode but varies completely in content. The brand reveal is the hook that gets the returning viewer to watch again — which brand is it this week? What are they looking for? Could the creators in today's episode be right for it?

---

### ROUND 1: "THE MATCH" (1:30 — 9:30)

The three contestants stand on their individual podiums. The brand brief appears on the screens: a clear description of the target audience the brand wants to reach, the type of creator they are looking for, and the budget range for the partnership.

The host builds tension by walking through each contestant's profile: their platform, their audience size, their key content category. The studio audience is being educated in real time about who each person is and why they might or might not be right for this brand.

Then: the DealStage AI runs the match. The screens animate — a visualization of the AI processing data, matching audience profiles, running compatibility algorithms. This animation lasts approximately 20 seconds and is designed to be visually dramatic. Three scores appear, one at a time, from highest to lowest.

The scores are revealed in reverse order — lowest first, highest last. The contestant with the highest score reacts with visible emotion. The contestant with the lowest score has to stand there while their number is revealed.

The lowest-scoring contestant is eliminated from the episode. They receive a consolation prize — a 3-month DealStage Pro subscription and a guaranteed profile review by the brand partner (who will look at their profile after the show and may reach out directly). This consolation structure matters: the show is not cruel. Elimination is a redirection.

The eliminated contestant speaks for 30 seconds: what they learned, what they will do differently. Then they exit. Two contestants remain.

---

### ROUND 2: "THE PITCH" (9:30 — 19:30)

The two remaining contestants pitch to the brand. Each has 90 seconds. No visual aids — just the contestant at the podium and the DealStage data screens behind them. The screens show their profile data; the contestant can reference it or ignore it, but the audience can see whether the data supports or undermines what they are saying.

After both pitches, the brand representative (present in the studio) asks one question of each contestant. The question is chosen in real time — not scripted — based on what the brand genuinely wants to know after hearing the pitch. This is the authenticity element that prevents the show from feeling like performance.

A studio audience reaction meter — a live applause-and-engagement monitor displayed on screen — registers the audience's response to each pitch in real time. This meter is not determinative: it does not eliminate anyone. But it is visible to the contestants and to the brand, and it creates an additional layer of information. Sometimes the audience falls in love with a pitch the brand is skeptical of. Sometimes the reverse. The gap between popular reaction and brand decision is one of the show's recurring dramatic engines.

After both pitches and questions, the brand makes a decision: one contestant advances to Round 3. The other is eliminated with the same consolation package as the Round 1 elimination.

---

### ROUND 3: "THE DEAL STAGE" (19:30 — 28:00)

The final contestant stands alone at "The Deal Stage" podium — a separate, central platform that is visually distinct from the Round 1 and Round 2 setup. The lighting changes. The studio audience quiets. The host steps back.

The brand representative stands at a podium on the opposite side of the stage. Between them, on the central screen, a number appears: the brand's opening offer. Let's say, for this example, $35,000 for a 6-week partnership campaign.

The contestant sees the number. The studio audience sees the number. The host reads it aloud.

Then the DealStage AI activates. The screens fill with data: market rate analysis for this brand category, the contestant's audience value metrics, comparable deal rates for creators with similar profiles, and the AI's recommendation. The recommendation is shown as a specific dollar amount with a confidence percentage:

**"DealStage recommends: Counter at $52,000 — 78% confidence this brand will accept."**

The contestant now has 30 seconds to decide: ACCEPT the $35,000 offer, or COUNTER with DealStage's recommended $52,000.

The clock runs on screen. The studio audience watches. The host is silent. This is the deal moment.

**If they ACCEPT:** They take the $35,000. The brand accepts. Confetti. The deal is real and binding. The contestant wins $35,000 in brand partnership value. The host notes that the AI thought they could have gotten more.

**If they COUNTER at $52,000:** The brand deliberates for 10 seconds on screen. Then they respond. If YES: the contestant wins $52,000 and the AI is validated. If NO: the contestant gets nothing. The deal is off the table entirely.

This risk/reward structure is the beating heart of the format. The "nothing" outcome when a counter is rejected is not bankruptcy — the contestant goes home with the consolation package and a real DealStage profile that brands can still access. But within the context of the episode, they stood at the podium and walked away with zero dollars when they had $35,000 on the table. That is the game show drama that audiences will talk about.

The AI's recommendation is always shown regardless of outcome. When the AI recommends a counter and the contestant ignores it and accepts the lower offer, the host notes it. When the AI recommends acceptance and the contestant counters anyway and loses, the audience knows the AI told them not to. The AI is not the villain and not the hero — it is the oracle that the contestant has to decide whether to trust.

---

### CLOSING (28:00 — 30:00)

The host recaps the deal outcome. The winner's final deal amount is shown on screen with the brand logo. The host tags: "Powered by DealStage — find out what brands would pay for your audience at thedealstage.com."

A brief preview of next week's brand partner. Credits.

## TWISTS AND FORMAT INNOVATIONS — VERSION B

### The Double Counter

Once per episode (at the host's discretion, exercised 3-4 times per season), after a contestant counters and the brand says no, the host can invoke "The Double Counter" — giving the brand the opportunity to come back with a lower counter-offer rather than a hard no. This prevents the all-or-nothing outcome in cases where the match is strong enough that the brand does not want to walk away entirely. It also adds a negotiation layer that educates the audience about how real deal-making works.

### The AI Override

In two episodes per season, after the AI delivers its recommendation, the brand can invoke "The AI Override" — they reveal their own internal budget ceiling before the contestant decides. This inverts the dynamic: the contestant now knows the maximum available and must decide whether to counter all the way to that number or take a smaller sure thing. The AI adjusts its recommendation in real time.

### The Audience Counter

In one episode per season, the studio audience votes on whether the contestant should accept or counter. The vote is shown to the contestant before they decide. They can follow the crowd or go against it. When the crowd says accept and the contestant counters successfully, the audience erupts. When the crowd says accept and the contestant counters and loses, the audience groans — because they told them.

### Rotating Celebrity Guest Hosts

The host is a permanent fixture of the show's identity. However, each episode features a celebrity co-host — a rotating roster that keeps the show fresh and generates press interest. Celebrity co-hosts do not make judging decisions; they are there to react, amplify, and represent the viewer's perspective. Target co-hosts: business personalities, pop culture figures, working creators, athletes, and entertainers who have had brand deal experience.

## HOW IT DRIVES DEALSTAGE PLATFORM SIGNUPS — VERSION B

Version B reaches a broader but less creator-specific audience than Version A. The conversion strategy is accordingly different: rather than converting creators who already understand the platform's value, the goal is to convert curious mainstream viewers into platform explorers.

**The "What's My Score?" Hook:** Every episode ends with a host tag that directs viewers to thedealstage.com to find out what brand would match with them. The DealStage free profile tool is the destination. For the 1-in-10 viewers who have some creator identity, this is an extremely compelling call to action. For the other 9-in-10, it is at minimum a curiosity click that builds the platform's audience data.

**The App Voting Mechanic:** Viewers who participate in the Audience Counter twist or the live finale vote must do so through the DealStage app. The app requires a free account. A percentage of those accounts convert to active users who explore the platform beyond the voting mechanic.

**Brand Partner Signups:** Each featured brand sees the DealStage platform in action during the episode. A percentage of those brands — small businesses in particular — visit the platform after the episode to inquire about listings. Brand signups are a significant revenue driver, and Version B's accessible format is more likely to attract small and mid-size brands than Version A's high-stakes structure.

**Weekly Frequency Advantage:** Version B airs 20 weeks per season. That is 20 weekly mentions of thedealstage.com to a 4-7M viewer audience. At even a 0.1% click-through rate, that is 8,000-14,000 platform visits per episode — 160,000-280,000 per season from broadcast mentions alone, before any social clip amplification is counted.

# COMBINED STRATEGY: RUNNING BOTH VERSIONS SIMULTANEOUSLY

The most powerful version of the DealStage television strategy is not choosing between Version A and Version B — it is running both.

Version B launches first on broadcast television and establishes the show's core premise for a mainstream audience. The short format, high-energy structure, and accessible concept builds brand recognition for "The Deal Stage" as a cultural entity. Version A launches 12-18 months later on a streaming platform, with Version B's proven audience as validation for the streaming deal. The two shows reinforce each other: Version B viewers who want more depth seek out Version A. The brand partner strategy is also complementary. Some brands (Progressive, Walmart, Target, T-Mobile) are better suited to Version B's mass-market reach. Several brands (American Express, Samsung, Sephora) want both — and a combined package can be structured to give them season-long presence across both shows for a premium investment.

The DealStage platform sits at the center of both shows as the connective tissue. A viewer who watches Version B on ABC and downloads the DealStage app to vote in the Audience Counter segment is the same potential user who may discover Version A on Netflix six months later and become a paid subscriber after seeing the depth of the platform's capabilities demonstrated in the long-form series. The television strategy is a funnel; the platform is the conversion.
