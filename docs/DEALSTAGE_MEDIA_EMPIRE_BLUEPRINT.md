## Journey 1: TALENT — "From Unknown to Unstoppable"

### The Story of Jordan

Jordan Blake woke up on a Tuesday morning in October 2024 to another empty inbox.

Not entirely empty — there was a credit card bill, a notification from Instagram that his latest workout reel had hit 80,000 views, and three automated responses from brands he'd cold-emailed three weeks ago. Two of the automated responses said "Thanks for reaching out! We'll be in touch." The third was a bounce. The brand's info@ address no longer existed.

Jordan had 180,000 Instagram followers. His engagement rate was 6.4%, which anyone in the creator economy would tell you is exceptional. He posted consistently — five times a week, morning and evening — and had built a highly specific niche around functional fitness for men aged 28 to 42: the guys who used to play college sports, now have desk jobs and two kids, and are trying to get back a version of themselves they thought they'd lost. His audience wasn't just large. It was loyal, targeted, and purchase-ready.

He knew this. His followers knew this. The brands he was emailing apparently did not.

For two years, Jordan had been running what he generously called a "brand outreach strategy." In reality, it was a ritual of hope and disappointment. He would spend Sunday evenings drafting emails — carefully personalized, he thought — to brands he admired. Protein supplement companies. Athletic apparel labels. Fitness tech startups. He'd find whatever contact he could on LinkedIn or through the brand's website. Usually that meant the generic info@ address, or sometimes the PR contact listed at the bottom of a press release from 18 months ago. He'd write the email, attach his media kit — a PDF he'd put together in Canva, last updated seven months ago — and hit send with a small surge of optimism that faded within 48 hours when silence confirmed, once again, that he was invisible.

His response rate was 2%. Not 2% closing into deals. 2% even acknowledging his existence.

Of the responses he did get, half were form letters. The other half were lowball offers — $300 for an Instagram post, $500 if he included a Story. He'd done the math: at those rates, and given how many hours he spent creating, editing, researching, and outreaching, he was earning less than minimum wage from brand deals. His best deal to date was $4,200 from a mid-tier protein brand, negotiated over six weeks of back-and-forth emails, for a campaign he'd been overdelivering on ever since because he was terrified of losing the relationship.

He had no contract template. He tracked his deals in a Google Sheet that had gotten out of control. He had no idea what other fitness creators with his audience size were charging. He had no idea which brands were actively looking for creators like him. He had no leverage, no data, and no framework.

He was, in the language of the creator economy, grinding. And grinding. And grinding.

Then his friend Dani — a lifestyle creator who had been quietly building a six-figure income from brand deals for the past year — texted him a link. "Just sign up," she wrote. "You'll understand in about five minutes."

The link was to DealStage.
