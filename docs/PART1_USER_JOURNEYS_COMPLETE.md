## JOURNEY 1A: TALENT

# Jordan's Story: From Ghosted to $340K

---

### THE BEFORE

The alarm goes off at 4:58 AM.

Jordan doesn't wait for it to hit 5:00. By 4:58, the mind is already running — editing cuts, caption ideas, the B-roll footage from yesterday's workout that might actually be something. The body hits the floor before the brain registers it's cold. This is the discipline that built 180,000 followers. This is also the discipline that is quietly killing Jordan.

The routine is sacred and brutal. An hour and a half of actual training — not content training, real training, the kind nobody sees — followed by 45 minutes of filming the content version. Then editing. Then the posting ritual: Instagram Reels, YouTube Shorts, TikTok, Pinterest. By 8:30 AM, the creative work is done. Then the business work begins. That's where the joy ends.

The spreadsheet opens on the laptop. It's called "Brand Outreach MASTER v14" — the 14 is not a coincidence. Thirteen previous versions became too unwieldy, too cluttered with dead ends and wrong email addresses and columns labeled "GHOSTED" highlighted in red. Version 14 has 847 rows. Each row is a brand. Each brand represents hours of research: finding the right contact name, verifying the email format, writing a personalized pitch, following up twice, logging the response (or lack thereof), and moving on. Over two years, Jordan has sent 1,694 cold emails — two follow-ups to each of the 847 brands. The response rate sits at 2.1%. Seventeen brands responded with genuine interest. Eleven of those went nowhere after the first call. Four became deals. Four deals in two years.

The math on those four deals is even more crushing when written down: $400, $350, $600, and $750. Total: $2,100 in brand deal revenue over 24 months. Jordan makes more than that in a single month from content monetization — but just barely. The monthly content revenue, between YouTube AdSense, Instagram bonuses, and a small Patreon, lands somewhere between $1,900 and $2,300 depending on the algorithms' mood. The rent in Silver Lake is $1,800. Not Beverly Hills. Not even West Hollywood. Silver Lake. And it goes up every year.

The lowball offers are their own specific kind of humiliation. Not because the brands are malicious — most of the marketing managers sending them seem genuinely nice, actually apologetic, almost — but because the gap between what is being offered and what is being asked is so vast it suggests the two parties aren't even in the same conversation. A supplement company with $40 million in annual revenue emails asking for a "brand ambassador package" — three dedicated posts, two Stories per week for a month, a product review video — for $150 and free product. A sportswear brand with retail presence in 2,000 stores asks for "authentic content creation" in exchange for a $75 gift card. A nutrition company that just closed a Series B wants exclusivity in the category for $200.

Jordan counter-proposes, politely, with market rate data. They ghost. Every time.

The Adidas situation is the one that almost broke everything.

It started well — unsolicited outreach from a real person at Adidas, a partnership manager who had clearly done her homework. She referenced specific posts. She talked about the audience demographic match. She used Jordan's name correctly in every email, which sounds like a low bar but isn't. Three rounds of negotiation over six weeks. Real numbers: $15,000 for a four-post campaign tied to a new running shoe launch. Jordan had already mentally deposited the money, already started thinking about finally building a proper home studio, maybe hiring someone part-time to help with editing. The fourth email never came. LinkedIn showed the partnership manager had left Adidas. The new contact inherited the inbox, saw nothing urgent about the situation, and moved on. Six weeks. Three rounds. $15,000. Gone.

It is a Tuesday morning, three weeks after the Adidas silence, that Jordan finally opens a Notes app and types: "Maybe I should just get a regular job." Not as a joke. Not as a social media caption about hustle culture. As a genuine, 3 AM-level reckoning with the question of whether this is sustainable. The number on the screen — $2,100 monthly income, $1,800 rent, $300 left for everything else — doesn't care how many followers you have. The algorithm doesn't pay rent. The engagement rate doesn't buy groceries.

The note sits there for four days. Jordan doesn't delete it. It is a real option.
